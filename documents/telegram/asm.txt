- Fewer required HCs! You need 4-6 HCs, with at least one from each core COMPETENCY, not each cornerstone course. Then, you can choose 2 of these HCs to get a score weighted 10x for transfer (only relevant to M26 and up)
- Updated resources of successful approaches and content from past papers

User_9cb696: Hi everyone!

Throughout the semester, I collected M27s thoughts, feedback and questions around the general topic of accommodations. I compiled them into these 5 questions, and I am happy to share the responses below authored by Missy Henry (Accommodations Specialist). Missy graciously took the effort to have it iteratively reviewed by several Minerva administrators including our Dean of Students, Dean of Student Services, Dean of Faculty, and Vice-President of Finance. The MyMinerva accommodations FAQ page (https://my.minerva.edu/student-services-new/disability-accommodations/) was also updated recently.

Question #1- Students would like to understand why accommodations cannot be applied retroactively. For example, a student has an episodic condition and did not suffer notable symptoms for months. Hence, they did not fill in the accommodations request module in Prepare when it was open. However, they suddenly had severe symptoms again at the start of the semester and was unable to get accommodations. 

Minerva’s accommodation process, as outlined in the Disability Accommodation Guide and the most recent FAQs, requires students to submit a request and complete the approval process before accommodations can be implemented. Once approved, accommodations are applied prospectively to provide equal access moving forward and are not intended to alter past academic outcomes. Under the ADA, institutions must consider reasonable accommodations for future academic work after a student has completed the request process. Retroactive academic adjustments are not legally required and may raise concerns about academic fairness.

Question #2-In relation to the above case, students have also been complaining that it is almost impossible to make up for missing the close date of an assignment. For example, a student experienced a strong unexpected relapse and was unable to submit the first assignment for a class, and decided to drop the class since they got 0s. I'm concerned that students on accommodations are more likely to miss the close date and would like to ask if there are statistics available on this. 

We are actively reviewing data on missed assignment close dates. Preliminary information suggests that accommodated and non-accommodated students miss close dates at similar rates, though a full analysis will be conducted once semester data are complete. The academic impact of missing an assignment varies by course and assignment weight, as determined by faculty and academic leadership to maintain academic standards. Faculty will use these findings to inform future course design and policy considerations.

Question #3-I understand students are given the option to request extra days for an assignment, but only if they write in before the due date. Many students, especially with ADHD, would only start assignments after the due date. If someone has 2 assignments due in a week, they might only start the second one after you submit the first one, and the second one would be ~3 days away. This also means that if you had an episodic disorder and had a relapse of some kind within those 3 days, you wouldn't be eligible for an extension. 

Minerva’s extension process requires students to request assignment extensions before the due date, a common and reasonable practice in post-secondary education and necessary for timely processing within academic systems. Accommodations are designed to provide limited, forward-looking flexibility when a student is actively working toward a deadline, rather than to apply after a deadline has passed. We recognize that conditions such as ADHD can affect time management and task initiation; therefore, we offer multiple forms of support, including individualized meetings, planning strategies, and alternative processes to facilitate timely extension requests where appropriate. In some cases, additional time beyond a limited extension may be counterproductive and is not considered a reasonable accommodation. Students with ongoing concerns are encouraged to engage in the accommodation process to proactively identify appropriate supports.
User_9cb696: Question #4-I am concerned about students who need psychiatric medication while on rotation due to varying restrictions on medications in different countries. I have been informed of occasions where, depending on the service needed, Cigna can take months to get someone an appointment, and the local hospital might only be able to provide small amounts of medication, forcing students to return to their home countries only to pick up medication. There have also been situations where students just ended up not getting their medications refilled because they struggled with the bureaucracy of the local healthcare system.

Students are responsible for ensuring that their medical care remains continuous during rotation, including access to all required medications. All eligible students must carry Minerva health insurance. Students are encouraged to plan, research local restrictions for each rotation city, and use available resources, such as the Pre-Departure Medical Assessment tool offered through Cigna, with guidance and country-specific information available on myMinerva, to anticipate potential challenges and plan accordingly. We encourage students who experience challenges with the use of the Cigna insurance to contact Student Services ([email]).

Question #5-When you switch to Independent Housing, Minerva adds the Independent Housing Credit to your bill, which removes the “accommodation” part of the Residential Services Fee. But if you receive scholarship beyond Tuition & Program Fees, Minerva reduces your scholarship by exactly the same amount as that credit, while your Expected Family Contribution stays the same (as long as your EFC is below Tuition + Program Fees). So for a high-need student whose aid was covering Minerva housing, going independent would mean paying more overall, because you keep paying about the same to Minerva and add new private rent costs instead of having housing covered on the bill. A student raised to me that this creates an unfair situation for students who were "forced" to get independent housing if they applied for housing accommodations (usually a single room) that Minerva was unable to provide. The student is wondering if Minerva could e.g. help increase financial aid or reduce student life costs to help cover rent. 

Minerva’s institutional scholarship applies only to charges billed directly by the University, including tuition, student services, and residential fees. It does not extend to off-campus housing costs. When a student moves to Independent Housing (IH), the housing portion of the Residential Services Fee is replaced with an IH credit, resulting in a proportional adjustment to the scholarship amount. All Minerva students, including those in IH, retain full access to university facilities, student life programming, and in-city support services. The University makes a good-faith, individualized effort to provide reasonable housing accommodation within the available housing inventory. When an approved accommodation cannot be fulfilled due to availability constraints, students are released from their Enrollment & Housing Commitment and may pursue IH as an alternative. Expanding Minerva housing to guarantee unlimited single rooms, or providing institutional aid in the form of cash disbursements for off-campus expenses, is not considered a reasonable or sustainable option.
