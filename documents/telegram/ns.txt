User_fd5d87: Rhali Attar (M24) is having his Qualifying Exams at UC Boulder this Friday, March 6. It is open to the public. Minerva students welcome! Thermal Signatures of Plume Deposits on Europa (see abstract below).

When: Friday March 6th at 17:15 UTC/9:15 am PST

Where: https://cuboulder.zoom.us/j/95693637229


Abstract:
Europa's icy surface contains a diverse set of geological features but the existence of cryovolcanic eruptions remains highly uncertain. In this study, we explore an alternative method for the detection of such activity. During an eruption, solid ice particles in plumes would be deposited on the surface and accumulate around the vent. We propose that these plume deposits have distinct thermophysical properties that would measurably affect diurnal surface temperature measurements. To explore this hypothesis, we simulate plume eruptions throughout the surface of Europa and use a thermal model to simulate their diurnal thermal variations. We then analyze the detectability of these thermal anomalies using Europa Clipper's E-THEMIS instrument. We also simulate the effects of ion sputtering on the thermophysical properties of the deposits to investigate how detectability is expected to change on geologic timescales. We find that detectability is likely constrained to plumes sourced from subsurface intrusions younger than 40 thousand years old.


Plain Language Summary:
Europa is one of the four largest moons of Jupiter and its surface is made of water ice, laying on top of a global subsurface ocean of liquid water. While Europa is extremely geologically diverse, we have yet to determine if it experiences volcanism. Unlike on Earth, Europa's volcanoes would erupt water vapor and ice particles; we call these cryovolcanoes or plumes. These could be sourced from the subsurface ocean or from water pockets in the ice crust, called subsurface intrusions. After an eruption, the area would be covered in ice particles which would form a plume deposit. In this study, we use models to explore whether the daily variations in temperature of these deposits would be different enough from the daily variations in temperature of Europa's surface that we could detect them. Moreover, Europa is being bombarded by high energy particles trapped in Jupiter's magnetic field which very slowly destroy the ice particles that form the plume deposits. So, we also model this process to analyze how long it would take for a plume deposit to no longer be detectable. We find that plumes sourced from subsurface intrusions would be detectable for around 40 thousand years.
