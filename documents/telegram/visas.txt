User_52ac9c: I never tried to transit through the US. However, I talked to some upperclassmen, to an airline (United) through their support chat, and also with [email] and got some information.
When you have a transit that is already in your flight ticket (you book only once and get all flights in just one ticket) the airlines will be able to see that the US is not your final destination. In this case, they might not allow you to board the plane (in this case, you might lose your flight. Or you might be offered the option to give up the second flight and just fly to the US as your final destination). If they do allow you to board, you might still be interviewed in the US by immigration officials (but they will probably let you go). United said in their support chat that F1 students are allowed to connect flights in the US with this visa, however United is one of those airlines who did not let students board the plane in the past.
An alternative students have done in the past is booking two separate tickets: one with the final destination in the US, and one from the US to the real final destination. In this case, the airline will not see that the US is not your final destination and will allow you to board (but be careful, because when flights are purchased separately, if the first flight delays and you miss the second flight because of that, you will not be refunded for the second flight or be placed on another flight, because they are separate). When you arrive in the US, you must enter the country (pass through immigration) because the US is your final destination. And then, you re-enter the airport again. In this case, you might also be interviewed by immigration officials (but they will probably let you go since you are properly documented and legal in the US).
[email] said that although we are allowed to connect flights with a F1 visa, there's no way to garantee that we will be alllowed to enter the US (or even board the plane). Because of that, they recommeded that, if we can, we could get a transit visa to connect a flight in the US, just to garantee everything goes smoothly.
Or, alternatively (and just to clarify: this alternative is NOT something Minerva recommended. I didn't ask Minerva anything about this option yet), you can also do that thing of purchashing the flight tickets separately and then passing through immigration and re-entering the airport to catch the second flight (that was purchased separately). In this case, you will probably be able to reach your final destination (since the airlines will not see you are not going to the US), but you might still be interviewed by officials
